I am a PhD student at the University of Savoie Mont Blanc in Chambéry, France. I am part of the Laboratory of Alpine Ecology (LECA). My research interest is theoretical ecology, and in particular I try to investigate how eco-evolutionary dynamics organize ecosystems using mathematical and numerical models. I'm particulary interested by the theory of multi-level selection and it represents a concept underlying my thesis works.

More precisely, I'm currently working on three projects:

Box tree moth project: Initially it was my Master's intership, but during my PhD I continue to work on, in order to publish. This project investigate whether the box tree moth, which is an invasive species in Europe, can persist thanks to spatial structure despite the fact that it can't persist locally because of overexploitation of the resource. We have shown that the spatial structure allows the persistence of the moth in an ecologically realistic range of parameters, despite a drastic reduction of resource biomass (box tree). Further, we show that moth dispersal distance plays a crucial role in the persistence, too long and there is global overexploitation and too short there is local overexploitation. Finally, we also show that under the coexistence regime the landscape is restructured in a way which favored the persistence of the moth; i.e the systems promotes its own persistence.

Eco-evo-foraging project: I'm working on a model of emerging community with two trophic levels (resource and consumer), where consumer can develop a foraging behavior by natural selection. This eco-evolutionary model allows to investigate how the adaptive foraging behavior can affect the diversification of the communities, then how the diversification can influence the stability. This work falls within the issue of complexity versus diversity (May 1973) but with evolving communities.

Major transition from mutualism to parasitism, role of the multi-level selection: The emergence and persistence of mutualism is a long standing issue. Several processes give a solution, often based on individual caracteristic like partner fidelity or punishment in social species. Multi-level selection gives also a solution, several works showed that mutualism can emerge thanks to spatial structure and the group-level selection resulting thereform. But these works often use models with specific assumption (no evolution of the dispersal distance, no parasitism). Here, I investigate how the mutualism can emerge from parasitism with a model coupling evolution of the interaction between species and evolution of the dispersal distance. A key hypothese is that mutualist individuals will have a short dispersal, and cheaters a long dispersal. Hence, the transition from independent parasitic individuals to dependent mutualistic individuals can start by a reduction of the dispersal distance which will increase the vertical transmision, and finally increase the selection toward mutualism interaction. However, the transition also requires competition between hosts in order to occur. Indeed, the transition occurs when mutualistic symbionts increase the density of hosts, which strengthens competition between hosts and disfavours parasitic host/symbiont pairs: mutualists create ecological conditions that allow their own spread. Therefore, the transition to mutualism may come from an eco-evolutionary feedback loop involving spatially structured population dynamics.
